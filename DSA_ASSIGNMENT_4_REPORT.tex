\section{Task 1 \textemdash{} Smart City Road Network Optimizer}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Problem Description}

The city is modelled as an undirected weighted graph with \(N\) intersections
and \(M\) roads. Every road carries two weights: a construction cost (used
for the MST) and a travel time (used for the shortest path). The class
implements graph validation, MST construction, shortest-path computation,
MST-vs-shortest-path comparison, and critical-road analysis.

\subsection{Approach}

\paragraph{Validation policy.} Five rules are enforced inside
\texttt{validateGraph}: no self-loops, no duplicate roads, non-negative
weights, valid node ids in \([0, N-1]\), and connectivity (checked by a BFS
from node 0). The first failing rule rejects the input with a specific reason.

\paragraph{MST.} \texttt{buildMST} uses Kruskal's algorithm: edges are
sorted by construction cost using a selection sort over edge indices, and a
DSU (with path compression and union by size) processes them in order. An
edge is accepted if its endpoints lie in different components and rejected
otherwise. The running MST cost is printed after every acceptance.

\paragraph{Shortest path.} \texttt{shortestPath} runs Dijkstra's algorithm
on the full graph (weighted by travel time) in the simple \(O(N^{2})\) form
that picks the next node by linearly scanning the distance array. The path
is reconstructed using a predecessor array and the per-hop travel time is
printed.

\paragraph{Comparison.} \texttt{compareNetworks} runs Dijkstra a second
time on the MST-only adjacency chain and reports the difference (absolute
and percentage) against the optimal shortest-path travel time.

\paragraph{Critical roads.} \texttt{criticalRoads} removes each MST edge in
turn, rebuilds the MST without it, and reports either that the graph
becomes disconnected, that the MST cost increases, or that a same-cost
alternative exists.

\subsection{Data Structures}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \texttt{Edge} \textemdash{} struct holding \texttt{u, v, cost, travelTime}.
    \item \texttt{vector<Edge> edges, mstEdges}.
    \item \texttt{vector<vector<pair<int,int>{>}{>} adjFull, adjMST} \textemdash{} adjacency chains weighted by travel time.
    \item DSU arrays \texttt{parent[], setSize[]} \textemdash{} reused across the build call and the rebuild loop inside \texttt{criticalRoads}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Task 2 \textemdash{} Logistics Dispatch Centre}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Problem Description}

A relief centre receives \(N\) aid requests, each with a unique
\texttt{requestID}, a priority in \([0, 100]\), and a submission timestamp.
The class supports a live max-heap dispatch queue with priority updates
and a top-k peek that does not modify the live heap, plus an in-place
randomised quicksort for shift-end record sorting on either field.

\subsection{Approach}

\paragraph{Data handling policy.} Duplicate IDs and out-of-range priorities
are rejected at input time with the offending value printed.
\texttt{updatePriority} additionally rejects unknown IDs and out-of-range
new priorities, leaving the heap unchanged in either case.

\paragraph{Live queue.} A hand-written max-heap is kept inside a
\texttt{vector<Request>} using the standard array indexing
(parent \(= (i-1)/2\), left \(= 2i+1\), right \(= 2i+2\)). The heap is
built using bottom-up heapify (Floyd's algorithm) in \(O(n)\) overall.
\texttt{dispatchNext} extracts the root, replaces it with the last element,
and sifts down. \texttt{updatePriority} locates the request by linear
scan, overwrites the priority, and sifts up or down depending on the change.

\paragraph{Sorting.} \texttt{sortRecords} implements randomised quicksort
with Lomuto partitioning: a random pivot is moved to \texttt{arr[hi]},
elements are partitioned around it, and the two halves are recursed on.
The sort is fully in-place; the only auxiliary space is the recursion
stack \(O(\log n)\). A fixed seed is used so the trace is reproducible.

\paragraph{Top-k.} \texttt{topKRequests(k)} copies \texttt{heap[]} into a
local buffer and runs \(k\) extract-max operations on the copy. The live
heap is never touched.

\subsection{Data Structures}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \texttt{Request} \textemdash{} struct with \texttt{requestID, priority, timestamp}.
    \item \texttt{vector<Request> heap} \textemdash{} the array-based max-heap.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Time and Space Complexity}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Operation} & \textbf{Time} & \textbf{Space} \\
\midrule
\texttt{validateInput()} & \(O(n^{2})\) & \(O(1)\) \\
\texttt{buildStructure()} (bottom-up heapify) & \(O(n)\) & \(O(n)\) (heap) \\
\texttt{dispatchNext()} & \(O(\log n)\) & \(O(1)\) \\
\texttt{updatePriority()} & \(O(n)\) (scan) \(+ O(\log n)\) (sift) & \(O(1)\) \\
\texttt{sortRecords()} (randomised quicksort) & \(O(n \log n)\) avg, \(O(n^{2})\) worst & \(O(\log n)\) auxiliary \\
\texttt{topKRequests(k)} & \(O(n + k \log n)\) & \(O(n)\) (temp copy) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Task 2. The \(O(\log n)\) auxiliary space for \texttt{sortRecords} comes solely from the recursion stack; no auxiliary arrays of size n are allocated.}
\end{table}
